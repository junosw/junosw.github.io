Tracking YouTube Picks

For the past 9–10 months, I’ve been working on a project that has real data backing it at this point: a system for tracking and evaluating MMA fight predictions from YouTube analysts.

A few people asked how it works, so I figured I’d write it up.

The Core Idea

At a high level, I built a fully automated system that:

  1. Pulls transcripts from YouTube prediction videos
  2. Identifies who each host is picking for every fight
  3. Assigns a confidence score to each pick, from 1 to 5
  4. Simulates betting based on those picks using real market odds

The goal is simple: figure out who actually knows what they’re talking about — not based on vibes, but on results over time.

How the Model Works

Once the prediction is extracted, it is assigned a confidence score from 1 to 5. That score determines bet size.

  • Base unit: $100
  • Confidence 1 → $100 bet ... Confidence 5 → $500 bet
  • The default is 1 and the majority of picks are rated a 1. 5's are very rare.

Then, after the event:

  • I capture the closing money line for each fight
  • I simulate a bet using that line

Example

Let’s say a predictor picks Yusef Zalal at -120. If the confidence score is 1, that becomes a $100 virtual bet.

If Zalal loses, the predictor loses $100.

On the other side, if another predictor picks Aljo at +140 and bets $100, they would win $140 if Aljo wins.

From there, I track total money wagered and calculate ROI for each predictor.

What I’m Tracking

Right now, I’m tracking 42 different YouTube predictors. However, not all of them have more than 100 picks yet, so just over half are showing up on the leaderboard at this point.

A few notes:

  • Not everyone posts every week
  • Some miss fights or picks
  • There’s definitely some noise and minor error

But over hundreds of picks, those inconsistencies will mostly wash out.

How I Choose Who to Track

I don’t include every MMA predictor on YouTube.

Selection is based on things like:

  • Video quality
  • Consistency
  • Audience size
  • Whether it’s something I’d realistically watch

There are plenty of smaller or lower-quality channels that I’ve intentionally excluded.

Methodology Caveats

There are plenty of valid criticisms a predictor might have:

  • “I made a pick, but I wouldn’t bet it.”
  • “I liked the fighter, but not at that line.”
  • “This was just a lean, not a play.”

All fair.

But over hundreds of predictions, those nuances tend to average out.

At scale, you’re either:

  • Making good calls relative to the market
  • Or you’re not

Why I Built This

This started from a simple question:

Who should I actually be listening to?

There are a lot of smart MMA analysts out there — but also a lot of noise.

This system helps answer a few things.

1. Who Provides Real Value?

If someone is consistently generating positive ROI against closing lines, they’re likely offering meaningful insight — not just entertainment.

2. Where Are the Lines Moving?

Many of these YouTubers have large audiences. Their picks can influence betting behavior.

I’ve noticed that:

  • Lines often move toward the more popular picks
  • Tracking these predictors can give early signals on market movement

3. Is the Consensus Smart?

I also track a consensus pick: if 65% or more of predictors pick one side, that becomes the consensus bet.

So far?

The consensus is not good.

It’s currently sitting around -1% ROI, meaning blindly following the crowd loses money over time.

How I Think About Watching These Shows

One important thing to call out: this is just data.

I know people have opinions — sometimes strong ones — about a lot of the creators I’m tracking. This project is not a judgment on anyone’s character, content quality, or ability to read fights, ability to make money betting on fights, etc. It is only a measurement of how the picks they post on Youtube perform against the market over time.

The data doesn’t care about our preferences or opinions. It’s simply measuring whether picks are right or wrong relative to the market.

Just because someone shows up as profitable here doesn’t mean I’m telling you that you should watch them. And just because someone is unprofitable doesn’t mean I’m saying you shouldn’t.

There are plenty of reasons to watch (or not watch) a show — entertainment value, personality, analysis style, etc. This model isn’t trying to capture any of that.

It’s purely tracking predictions because I wanted to know one thing: who is actually right relative to the market over time.

What you do with that information is entirely up to you.

Closing Thoughts

At the end of the day, this project isn’t about calling people out — it’s about adding clarity, for me and hopefully for others who are interested in MMA betting and analysis.

There’s a ton of MMA content out there. This is just one way to bring a little objectivity to it.

If there’s a predictor you like that I’m not tracking, let me know — I’m always open to expanding the dataset. I plan to continue tracking and updating this over time - more data the better.
